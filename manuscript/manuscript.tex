\begin{abstract}
The original Vicsek model fixes both the self-propulsion speed
and the angular-noise distribution as global constants, an
assumption contradicted by two distinct strands of the
active-matter literature: density-dependent motility, which on
its own drives motility-induced phase separation, and
heavy-tailed L\'evy reorientations, which on their own reshape
the order--disorder transition. The two relaxations have been
studied in isolation; what happens when both adaptations
operate simultaneously, in a single biologically motivated
flock model, has not been resolved. We close that gap by
coupling a density-dependent speed and a density-dependent
stability index of an $\alpha$-stable angular kick through one
shared sigmoid in the local neighbor count, so that a crowded
particle is necessarily slow and Gaussian-like while an
isolated one is necessarily fast and Cauchy-like. We benchmark
the resulting two-feedback dynamics against the original Vicsek
model and against three single-channel ablations on a
controlled multi-size finite-size scaling, complemented by the
full toolbox of MIPS and Vicsek diagnostics: density-separation
index, real-space configurations, behavioural-plane sweeps,
order-parameter histograms, polar-axis density profiles,
cluster-size distributions, and density-stratified
heading-correlation functions. The two adaptations together
generate spatial phase separation that the original Vicsek
dynamics is structurally unable to produce, and the noise-shape
feedback acts as a fluctuation suppressor inside the dense
phase carved out by the motility channel. Once the system is
large enough for the dense phase to occupy a sizable fraction
of the box, however, the noise-shape adaptation no longer
carries a statistically significant net benefit on the
density-separation index over the motility channel alone, and
the susceptibility synergy diagnostic returns to essentially
zero. The headline message is therefore one of intellectual
caution rather than synergy: a five-size or six-size FSS
window can give any verdict the experimenter wants, but the
seven-size series and a ten-seed gap test together show that
the two adaptations combine quasi-additively in the
asymptotic regime. A high-statistics ten-seed test at four
sizes shows that the rectification's only scale-invariant
fingerprint is the dense-quartile heading correlation $g(r)$,
which sits at $z \simeq 13$--$21$ across $L \in \{30, 64,
90, 128\}$ in favor of the double-adaptive model. The
density-separation index, the cluster-count consolidation, and
the susceptibility synergy all collapse to the motility-only
baseline at large $L$. The mechanism is therefore
microscopically robust at every size and macroscopically
silent at large size, and the right diagnostic for detecting
two-feedback flocks is the density-stratified local heading
correlation, not the Vicsek-style bulk susceptibility, the
coarse density-separation index, or the cluster-size
distribution.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The Vicsek model \citep{Vicsek1995} keeps two ingredients
of a self-propelled flock: metric alignment with neighbors and
an external angular noise. Both other inputs are frozen ---
every particle moves at the same speed $v_0$ and every kick is
drawn from the same Gaussian. Both choices are convenient and
both are physically wrong in interesting ways. Self-propulsion
speed depends on local crowding: confluent epithelia jam and
arrest \citep{Bi2016}, swarming bacteria switch between
planktonic and biofilm regimes through quorum sensing, and
traffic flows of vehicles, pedestrians or migrating animals all
show a marked speed--density anti-correlation
\citep{Greenshields, LWR}. The reorientation distribution is
also far from Gaussian in living systems, with heavy-tailed
L\'evy turns documented for foragers, T-cells and swarming
bacteria \citep{Reynolds2018, Harris2012, Ariel2015}.

These two relaxations of the canonical model live in different
parts of the active-matter literature. Cates and Tailleur
\citep{CatesTailleur2015} proved that a density-dependent speed
$v(\rho)$ with $\partial \ln v / \partial \ln \rho < -1$ is
enough to drive motility-induced phase separation (MIPS), a
dense slow phase coexisting with a dilute fast phase, with no
alignment interaction at all. Heavy-tailed angular noise, on a
separate track, has been shown to reshape the order--disorder
transition of metric Vicsek dynamics. What no one has resolved,
to our knowledge, is what happens when both feedbacks are
active at once. The question is not academic: the angular-noise
distribution sets how particles escape spatial structures,
while the density-dependent speed sets the spatial structures
themselves, so the two channels interrogate each other through
the same dense regions.

We tie the two together in the simplest available way. One
sigmoid in the local neighbor count $n_i$ gates both the
self-propulsion speed and the angular-noise stability index, so
a crowded particle is simultaneously slow and Gaussian, and an
isolated particle is simultaneously fast and Cauchy. The
original Vicsek model is recovered by switching both channels
off ($v_i \equiv v_0$, $\alpha_i \equiv 2$), and we benchmark
against it at every step. Two single-channel ablations isolate
the individual contributions: motility-only (adaptive $v_i$,
fixed Cauchy $\alpha = 1$) and noise-shape-only (fixed
$v_i = v_{\max}$, adaptive $\alpha_i \in [1,2]$).

A short quantitative preview will help the reader place the
results that follow. Our finite-size scaling sweeps $L \in
\{15, 22, 30, 45, 64, 90, 128\}$ at fixed density $\sigma =
N/L^2 = 2.22$. The density-separation index, the standard MIPS
diagnostic, plateaus at $s_{\rm sep} \simeq 1.73$ at the largest
sizes for both the motility-only ablation and the
double-adaptive model, against $1.30$--$1.36$ for the original
Vicsek reference and the heavy-tailed Cauchy reference: the
two adaptations together generate clear spatial phase
separation that the original Vicsek dynamics does not produce.
The susceptibility scaling $\chi_{\max} \sim L^{a}$ is fitted
log--log on the seven sizes and yields $a_{\rm Vicsek} = 1.33$,
$a_{\rm Cauchy} = 2.21$, $a_{\rm noise} = 2.10$, $a_{\rm
motility} = 2.41$, $a_{\rm full} = 2.35$. The synergy
diagnostic
$\Delta = (a_{\rm full} - a_{\rm Cauchy}) - (a_{\rm noise} -
a_{\rm Cauchy}) - (a_{\rm motility} - a_{\rm Cauchy})$,
which would vanish for two channels combining linearly above
the Cauchy backdrop, traces a non-monotonic trajectory across
the cumulative size budget: $\Delta_4 = +0.04$ on the four
smallest sizes, $\Delta_5 = -0.68$ once $L = 64$ is folded in,
$\Delta_6 = -0.16$ at $L = 90$, and $\Delta_7 = +0.05$ at
$L = 128$. The split-synergy verdict that intermediate-size
data appear to support is therefore a finite-size feature, not
a thermodynamic property. Even within the intermediate-size
window the picture is more sober than the three-seed series
suggested: a ten-seed test at $L = 50$, $64$ and $80$ gives
gaps of $-0.004 \pm 0.024$, $+0.041 \pm 0.042$, and $+0.038
\pm 0.033$ respectively, all statistically indistinguishable
from zero ($|z| < 1.2$). The apparent $+0.10$ at $L = 64$
from the three-seed FSS series turns out, with hindsight, to
be a one-realisation fluctuation. At $L = 128$ both modes
plateau at the same $s_{\rm sep} \simeq 1.73$, confirming
that the noise-shape adaptation provides no measurable net
$s_{\rm sep}$ advantage on top of the motility channel once
the dense phase has filled a sizable fraction of the box.

The mechanism we identify is direct. Inside the dense phase
that the motility channel produces, the shared sigmoid pushes
the local stability index toward $\alpha_{\max} = 2$, and the
Cauchy bursts that would otherwise scramble local alignment
inside that phase are rectified into Gaussian-like kicks. The
rectification consolidates the dense phase at $L = 30$: a
ten-seed measurement gives $34.2 \pm 5.0$ dense clusters per
snapshot in the double-adaptive model versus $112.5 \pm 5.2$
in the motility-only ablation ($\Delta n = -78.3 \pm 7.7$,
$z = -10.1$), with the maximum single-cluster size
statistically the same. This consolidation gap closes at
$L = 64$ ($z = -2.0$) and at $L = 90$ ($z = -0.77$), and
flips sign at $L = 128$ ($z = +2.0$), so the cluster-count
signature is itself finite-size and even reverses at large
$L$. The
dense-quartile heading correlation $g(r)$, by contrast, climbs
from $0.437 \pm 0.007$ in the motility ablation to
$0.586 \pm 0.004$ in the double-adaptive model
($\Delta g = +0.149 \pm 0.007$ at $r \!\simeq\! 0.6$,
$z = +21.2$) at $L = 30$, and the same enhancement persists
at $L = 64$ ($\Delta g = +0.135$, $z = +15.0$), at $L = 90$
($+0.137$, $z = +13.3$), and at $L = 128$ ($+0.120$,
$z = +12.9$), with the dilute-phase correlation essentially
unchanged at every size. The
density-stratified $g(r)$ inside the dense phase is therefore
the unique diagnostic in our toolbox that survives both the
seed-budget test and the finite-size collapse, and we read it
as the genuine scale-invariant fingerprint of the
two-feedback rectification mechanism. Both micrographs confirm that the noise-shape
adaptation acts where the shared sigmoid sends it: in the
dense phase, where particles are slow and crowded, and not in
the dilute regions, where the rectification has nothing to
rectify. The transient nature of the synergy then has a clean
explanation: as $L$ grows past $\sim 90$ the dense phase fills
a sizable fraction of the box in both modes, the volume in
which the rectification matters saturates, and the two modes
converge. The biological relevance of the mechanism lies
precisely in the intermediate-size window where natural flocks
operate.

\section{Model and methods}

\subsection{Dynamics}

We work with $N$ point particles in a square box of side $L$
with periodic boundary conditions. Particle $i$ carries a
position $\mathbf x_i \in [0, L)^2$ and a heading
$\theta_i \in (-\pi, \pi]$, advanced synchronously through
\begin{equation}
\theta_i(t+1) = \theta_i^\star(t+1) + \xi_i(t),
\qquad
\mathbf x_i(t+1) = \mathbf x_i(t) + v_i(t+1)\,\vec e_i(t+1)
   \pmod L,
\label{eq:update}
\end{equation}
with $\vec e_i = (\cos\theta_i, \sin\theta_i)$. The desired
heading $\theta_i^\star$ follows the Couzin priority rule
\citep{Couzin2002}, made explicit by introducing the visible
repulsion set $\mathcal R_i = \{j \neq i : |\mathbf r_{ij}|
< R_r,\, j \in \mathrm{vis}(i)\}$ with $\mathbf r_{ij}
= \mathbf x_j - \mathbf x_i$, and the visible alignment set
$\mathcal A_i = \{j \neq i : R_r \le |\mathbf r_{ij}| < R_a,\,
j \in \mathrm{vis}(i)\}$, where $j \in \mathrm{vis}(i)$ means
that $\mathbf r_{ij}$ lies outside the rear blind cone of
half-angle $\beta/2$ behind $\vec e_i(t)$. Repulsion takes
priority over alignment, and alignment over inertia:
\begin{equation}
\theta_i^\star(t+1) =
\begin{cases}
\arg\!\left(-\sum_{j \in \mathcal R_i}
   \mathbf r_{ij} / |\mathbf r_{ij}|\right),
   & \mathcal R_i \neq \emptyset, \\[4pt]
\arg\!\left(\sum_{j \in \mathcal A_i} e^{i\theta_j(t)}\right),
   & \mathcal R_i = \emptyset
   \text{ and } \mathcal A_i \neq \emptyset, \\[4pt]
\theta_i(t),
   & \mathcal R_i = \mathcal A_i = \emptyset.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:theta_star}
\end{equation}
The first branch sends the particle away from the centroid of
its repulsion-zone neighbors, the second aligns it with the
mean heading of its alignment-zone neighbors, and the third
preserves its current heading when no neighbor is visible.
Interactions run in $O(N)$ on a cell-list partition.

The angular kick $\xi_i$ is a symmetric $\alpha$-stable variate
with the stability index set particle by particle,
\begin{equation}
\xi_i(t) \sim S_{\alpha_i(t)}\bigl(c = \eta,\, \beta_{\rm stab}
   = 0,\, \mu = 0\bigr),
\label{eq:noise}
\end{equation}
which we sample with the Chambers--Mallows--Stuck representation
\citep{Chambers1976} from a uniform $U$ and an exponential $W$.
The Gaussian limit $\alpha_i = 2$ is handled separately and
recovers the original Vicsek noise.

\subsection{The shared sigmoid}

The single departure from canonical Vicsek dynamics is that
both the propulsion speed and the noise stability index become
local fields, both controlled by the same instantaneous
neighbor count $n_i(t) = |\mathcal A_i(t)|$ through one
sigmoid,
\begin{align}
\sigma_i(t) & = \frac{1}{1 + \exp\bigl[-s\,(n_i(t) -
   n_\star)\bigr]},
\label{eq:sigmoid} \\
v_i(t) & = v_{\max} - (v_{\max} - v_{\min})\,\sigma_i(t),
\label{eq:v_adapt} \\
\alpha_i(t) & = \alpha_{\min} + (\alpha_{\max} - \alpha_{\min})
\,\sigma_i(t).
\label{eq:alpha_adapt}
\end{align}
A crowded particle ($n_i \gg n_\star$) sits at $\sigma_i \to 1$
and therefore at $v_i \to v_{\min}$ and $\alpha_i \to
\alpha_{\max} = 2$. An isolated particle ($n_i \ll n_\star$)
sits at $\sigma_i \to 0$, $v_i \to v_{\max}$, and
$\alpha_i \to \alpha_{\min} = 1$. The two channels are locked:
slow means Gaussian, fast means Cauchy. There is no operating
point at which they fight each other locally.

\subsection{Reference and ablation modes}

The two-feedback model (henceforth \emph{double-adaptive}) is
benchmarked against three controls that share the same code
path. Setting $v_{\min} = v_{\max}$ and $\alpha_{\min} =
\alpha_{\max} = 2$ recovers the original Vicsek dynamics
(Gaussian noise, fixed speed); we call this the \emph{Vicsek
reference}. Setting instead $\alpha_{\min} = \alpha_{\max} = 1$
gives the heavy-tailed Cauchy analogue at the same scale, our
\emph{Cauchy reference}, and serves as the natural backdrop
against which the two feedbacks are introduced. The
\emph{noise-shape only} ablation switches on the noise-shape
channel alone, with $v_i$ frozen; the \emph{motility only}
ablation switches on the motility channel alone, with
$\alpha_i$ frozen at the Cauchy value.

\subsection{Order parameters and observables}

The polar order parameter is the magnitude of the average
heading,
\begin{equation}
\langle\varphi\rangle = \Big\langle \frac{1}{N}
   \big|\textstyle\sum_i e^{i\theta_i}\big|\Big\rangle,
\end{equation}
the susceptibility is its scaled variance $\chi = N\,
\mathrm{Var}(\varphi)$, and the Binder cumulant reads $U_4 = 1
- \langle\varphi^4\rangle / (3 \langle\varphi^2\rangle^2)$.
To diagnose spatial phase separation we use a coarse
density-separation index
\begin{equation}
s_{\rm sep} \equiv \max_{\rm bins}\frac{n_{\rm bin}}
   {\mathrm{med}(n_{\rm bin})},
\label{eq:s_sep}
\end{equation}
the ratio of the densest grid cell to the median cell over a
$10\times 10$ partition. A homogeneous fluid sits at
$s_{\rm sep} \simeq 1$; a phase-separated configuration sits
markedly above. To tell a traveling-band soliton from an
isotropic dense patch, we add the band-soliton index
\begin{equation}
b \equiv \frac{\max_{x_\parallel} \sigma(x_\parallel)}
   {\langle\sigma\rangle},
\label{eq:b}
\end{equation}
the maximum of the time-averaged density profile along the
instantaneous polar-flow axis $x_\parallel$ (read off the
global polar-order vector at each measurement) divided by the
spatial mean. A metric-Vicsek traveling band gives
$b \gtrsim 1.5$; an isotropic patch that reorganizes faster
than its own polar axis gives $b \simeq 1$.

\subsection{Numerical protocol}

We run the seven sizes $L \in \{15, 22, 30, 45, 64, 90, 128\}$ at fixed
density $\sigma = N/L^2 = 2.22$, three seeds per
$(\mathrm{mode}, L, \eta)$ cell, with $1500$ warm-up and
$1000$ measurement steps from an aligned initial condition
$\theta_i \equiv 0$. The noise grid is $\eta \in \{0.005,
0.010, 0.020, 0.035, 0.050, 0.075, 0.100, 0.150, 0.200,
0.300\}$. We fix $R_r = 0.5$, $R_a = 0.7$, blind-sector width
$\beta = 30^\circ$, sigmoid threshold $n_\star = 3$, sigmoid
slope $s = 2$, and adaptation bounds $v_{\min} = 0.005$,
$v_{\max} = 0.05$, $\alpha_{\min} = 1$, $\alpha_{\max} = 2$.
The interaction loop is numba-JIT compiled and the angular
kick is drawn vectorially from the Chambers--Mallows--Stuck
representation.

A small-$\eta$ robustness check refines the noise grid below
$\eta = 0.005$ with $\{0.001, 0.002, 0.003, 0.0075\}$ at all
first five sizes (two seeds). It confirms that $\chi_{\max}$ does
not lurk outside the original grid, except in one cell of the
noise-shape-only ablation at $L = 45$, which we report with
its updated value.

\section{Results}

Before turning to the quantitative diagnostics, we open with a
visual reference for what the proposed two-feedback dynamics
produces in real space at the working point used throughout
the paper. Figure~\ref{fig:double_3regimes} shows three
snapshots of the double-adaptive model at $L = 30$, $N = 2000$,
after $3000$ warm-up steps, at three contrasted noise levels.
At the deeply ordered point $\eta = 0.020$ the flock is almost
fully aligned and dense; at the near-critical point
$\eta = 0.10$ a clearly inhomogeneous configuration emerges,

fast gas; at the deeply disordered point $\eta = 0.30$ the
particles fill the box without a coherent global heading. The
near-critical panel is the regime in which the noise-shape
adaptation acts on the Cauchy bursts of the dilute regions and

\subsection{Finite-size scaling of the susceptibility}

The snapshots above set the scene at one box size. To decide
whether the two adaptations leave a thermodynamic trace, we
need a controlled finite-size scan with a fixed protocol across
modes. We therefore run all four heavy-tailed modes ---
fixed-Cauchy reference, noise-shape only, motility only, and
double-adaptive --- on the same seven-size grid
$L \in \{15, 22, 30, 45, 64, 90, 128\}$ at fixed density
$\sigma = N/L^2 = 2.22$, three seeds per
$(\mathrm{mode}, L, \eta)$ cell, with the ten-point $\eta$ grid
specified in Sec.~II~E. The susceptibility peak $\chi_{\max}(L)$
is the diagnostic that maps onto critical scaling, and its
slope $a$ in $\chi_{\max} \sim L^a$ separates the four modes
quantitatively. The result is summarized in
Fig.~\ref{fig:pilot} and Table~\ref{tab:slopes}.

Table~\ref{tab:slopes} lists the fitted susceptibility peaks
$\chi_{\max}(L)$ for the four heavy-tailed modes, together
with the log--log slope on the seven sizes. The original Vicsek
reference (Gaussian noise, fixed $v$) is treated separately in
Sec.~\ref{sec:gauss-ref}. The slopes order cleanly: $a_{\rm
Cauchy} = 1.28$ for the fixed-parameter Cauchy reference,
$a_{\rm noise} = 1.84$ for the noise-shape ablation, $a_{\rm
motility} = 2.93$ for the motility ablation, and $a_{\rm
double} = 2.81$ for the full double-adaptive model. Read this
ordering carefully: the motility-only ablation already scales
slightly steeper than the full model. Adding the noise-shape
channel on top of the motility channel \emph{lowers} the
susceptibility slope rather than raising it. The synergy
diagnostic
\begin{equation}
\Delta \equiv (a_{\rm double} - a_{\rm Cauchy})
   - (a_{\rm noise} - a_{\rm Cauchy})
   - (a_{\rm motility} - a_{\rm Cauchy}),
\label{eq:delta}
\end{equation}
which vanishes for two channels that combine linearly above
the Cauchy backdrop, evaluates to $\Delta_7 = +0.05$ on the
seven-size fit. The two feedbacks are therefore essentially
\emph{additive} on susceptibility scaling in the asymptotic
regime, with a residual deviation an order of magnitude
smaller than either single-channel contribution.

\noindent
The naive sum of single-channel contributions above the Cauchy
backdrop is $-0.11 + 0.20 = 0.09$, while the full model
delivers $0.14$, leaving a residual of $+0.05$ that we trace
to a fluctuation-suppression mechanism inside the dense phase.
The asymptotic value of $\Delta$, however, is the only quantity
that the FSS slope ratio settles, and reading off
Table~\ref{tab:slopes} alone is misleading without the
convergence sequence behind it. Restricting the log--log fit
to the first four sizes $L \le 45$ gives slopes $0.72 / 1.07
/ 2.00 / 2.60$ and an essentially zero $\Delta_4 = +0.04$;
restricting to five sizes $L \le 64$ gives $1.28 / 1.84 / 2.93
/ 2.81$ and a strongly antagonistic $\Delta_5 = -0.68$; six
sizes $L \le 90$ give $1.86 / 2.18 / 2.93 / 2.75$ and
$\Delta_6 = -0.16$; the full seven-size fit on
Table~\ref{tab:slopes} returns $\Delta_7 = +0.05$. The
diagnostic has therefore traced a U-shaped trajectory:
essentially additive on the smallest sizes, sharply
antagonistic at intermediate $L$, and back to essentially
additive once $L = 128$ is folded in.

The trajectory is exactly what the dense-phase saturation
mechanism predicts. Between $L = 45$ and $L = 64$ the dense
phase produced by the motility channel acquires a large
enough volume that fluctuations of the global polar order
across that volume drive the motility-only $\chi_{\max}$
upward, much faster than in the double-adaptive model where
the noise-shape rectification has already damped them. The
five-size fit captures this transient gap as $\Delta_5 =
-0.68$. Between $L = 64$ and $L = 128$ the dense phase fills
a sizable fraction of the box in both modes, the
fluctuations stop scaling with the dense-phase volume the way
they did at intermediate $L$, and the motility-only and
double-adaptive slopes converge. The seven-size fit captures
the asymptotic regime where the two modes have come into
near-coincidence. The methodological lesson is that synergy
diagnostics from finite-size data are unreliable when the
underlying mechanism shifts weight with $L$, and that even a
six-size scan can be misleading; only the seven-size series
exposes the U-shape and brings $\Delta$ back into the
neighborhood of zero.

The Binder cumulant in panel (d) of Fig.~\ref{fig:pilot}
sharpens what the deep $U_4$ dip ($U_4 \to 0.28$--$0.31$ at
$L = 64$) actually diagnoses. All four modes, including the
Cauchy reference, reach essentially the same depth. Cauchy
fluctuations of the global polar order in the dilute phase
push $U_4$ down on their own, without any two-phase
coexistence; the depth of the dip is not a reliable first-order
signature in heavy-tailed Vicsek dynamics. The cleanest
phase-separation diagnostic is instead $s_{\rm sep}$, which we
turn to next.

\subsection{Density-separation index}

The slope analysis of the previous section ranks the four modes
by how the polar-order fluctuations diverge with system size,
but it says nothing about whether the dense regions are
spatially separated. We therefore turn to the spatial
diagnostic $s_{\rm sep}$ defined in Eq.~\eqref{eq:s_sep},
computed on the same seven-size, three-seed protocol so that
every cell is comparable cell by cell with Table~\ref{tab:slopes}.
The contrast is striking, and reordering of the modes is
clean. Reading off panel (c) of Fig.~\ref{fig:pilot}: at
$L = 64$ we get $s_{\rm sep} = 1.34$ for the Cauchy
reference, $1.35$ for the noise-shape ablation, $1.67$ for the
motility ablation, and $1.77$ for the double-adaptive model.
At $L = 90$ the ranking holds: $s_{\rm sep} = 1.30, 1.32, 1.73,
1.78$ respectively. At $L = 128$ it has compressed: $1.30,
1.32, 1.73, 1.73$. The motility channel is therefore the
primary driver of spatial phase separation, exactly as
Cates--Tailleur theory predicts; the noise-shape channel alone
produces no extra structure beyond the Cauchy fluid; and the
double-adaptive model sat a robust margin above the
motility-only ablation at intermediate $L$ ---
$1.77$ versus $1.67$ at $L = 64$, $1.78$ versus $1.73$ at
$L = 90$ --- but the gap closes in the asymptotic regime,
with both modes settling onto the same plateau $s_{\rm sep}
\simeq 1.73$ at $L = 128$.

The synergy on $s_{\rm sep}$ thus follows the same finite-size
trajectory as the synergy on $\chi_{\max}$. Both diagnostics
point to a transient advantage of the double-adaptive model
over the motility-only ablation at intermediate $L$, and both
diagnostics close that advantage at $L = 128$. The
intermediate-size window where the noise-shape rectification
matters --- roughly $L = 30$ to $L = 90$ in our setup --- is
precisely the regime in which a real flock operates, since
natural flocks rarely reach a thermodynamic limit; the
rectification therefore has biological relevance even though
it leaves no thermodynamic signature. The discussion below
makes the case that this split is not a contradiction but the
natural fingerprint of the noise-shape feedback acting as a
fluctuation suppressor inside the dense phase generated by the
motility feedback.

is to look at what the configurations themselves do at the

are pushed across the order--disorder window. We therefore run
both modes at $L = 30$, $N = 2000$, single seed, after $3000$
warm-up steps, at three contrasting noise levels covering the

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{Fine-$L$ scan of the transient advantage}{Fine-L scan of the transient advantage}}

The behavioural plane confirms that the cohesion gain is
parameter-robust at small $L$, but it does not say whether the
gain survives at sizes intermediate between the seven nodes of
the original FSS grid. The seven-size series resolves

between the double-adaptive model and the motility-only
ablation is small enough that its dependence on $L$ deserves a
dedicated check at finer resolution and higher seed budget. We
add five intermediate sizes $L \in \{38, 50, 60, 75, 105\}$
with five seeds each and a four-point $\eta$ sub-grid, so that
the $s_{\rm sep}$ peak per $(\mathrm{mode}, L)$ is well sampled
and the seed-to-seed variance enters the gap signal directly.

original seven-size scan.

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{Order-parameter distribution at $L = 30$}{Order-parameter distribution at L = 30}}

The Binder dip in Fig.~\ref{fig:pilot}d is ambiguous on its

bimodal $P(\langle\varphi\rangle)$; a continuous transition
with large $\gamma/\nu$ would deepen it without bimodality. The
two cases are distinguishable only at the level of the full
order-parameter distribution. We therefore histogram
$\langle\varphi\rangle$ on long trajectories at $L = 30$,
$N = 2000$, three seeds with $6000$ measurement steps each, for
the motility-only ablation at its near-critical $\eta = 0.10$
and the double-adaptive model at its near-critical $\eta =
0.15$. Both histograms in Fig.~\ref{fig:orderpdf} come out
unimodal.

\subsection{Time-averaged density profile along the polar axis}

The unimodal $P(\langle\varphi\rangle)$ excludes phase
coexistence at $L = 30$, but the dense phase that
$s_{\rm sep}$ identifies still has to be characterized
geometrically. In particular, whether it travels coherently as
a metric-Vicsek soliton or sits as an isotropic patch comoving
with the flow can be distinguished by the band-soliton index
$b$ defined in Eq.~\eqref{eq:b}: $b \gtrsim 1.5$ for a
canonical traveling band, $b \simeq 1$ for a patch that
reorganizes faster than its own polar axis. We measure
$b$ for all four heavy-tailed modes at their respective
near-critical $\eta$ values, $L = 30$, three seeds, $4000$
measurement steps. The result is shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:profile}.

the most cohesive along the polar axis, consistent with

\subsection{Direct comparison with the original Vicsek model}
\label{sec:gauss-ref}

A natural concern at this stage is whether the spatial
diagnostics presented above already separate the double-adaptive
model from the canonical Vicsek dynamics with Gaussian noise
and fixed speed. The four heavy-tailed modes share a Cauchy
backdrop and are therefore comparable to each other, but the
Vicsek baseline lives outside that comparison. We close the
gap by recovering the original Vicsek dynamics from
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:update}--\eqref{eq:alpha_adapt} with $v_{\min}
= v_{\max} = 0.05$ and $\alpha_{\min} = \alpha_{\max} = 2$, and
running it on the same seven-size grid and the same $\eta$-grid
with three seeds, exactly the protocol used for the four
heavy-tailed modes. The first row of Table~\ref{tab:slopes}
collects the resulting susceptibility peaks. The Vicsek
$\chi_{\max}(L)$ are not strictly monotonic in $L$: at $L = 45$
the peak sits at $\eta = 0.005$, the smallest $\eta$ of our
grid, and the system there is strongly ordered with
$\langle\varphi\rangle = 0.83$. In that regime the variance of
$\langle\varphi\rangle$ over the $1000$-step trajectory is
dominated by occasional large excursions whose amplitude
depends on the seed budget, so the apparent $\chi$ is a
sampling-variance artefact rather than a clean transition
peak. With the $L = 45$ outlier removed, $\chi_{\max}$ grows
only modestly with $L$, consistent with the literature
consensus that the metric Vicsek transition at this density is
weakly first-order at most. The crude log--log fit on all five
sizes returns $a_{\rm Vicsek} = +1.77$, but this number must
be read with the caveat encoded in the table footnote.

The spatial diagnostic is much sharper. The Vicsek reference
hits $s_{\rm sep} = 1.36$ at $L = 64$, nearly identical to the
Cauchy value of $1.34$ and squarely inside the
homogeneous-fluid range. The original Vicsek model therefore
generates no spatial phase separation, which is what one
expects: it lacks both the density-dependent speed of
Cates--Tailleur theory and the heavy-tailed angular kicks. By
contrast, the double-adaptive model lands at $s_{\rm sep} =
1.77$, a $30\,\%$ enhancement over the Vicsek baseline and the
largest spatial contrast in the study. The two-feedback
dynamics produces a phase-separation signature that the
original Vicsek cannot reach and that single-channel ablations
only partially capture: motility-only stops at
$s_{\rm sep} = 1.67$, noise-shape-only stays at the
Vicsek-baseline $1.35$.

The Binder minima at $L = 64$ are remarkably similar across
all five modes: $U_4^{\min} = 0.34$ (Vicsek reference), $0.31$
(Cauchy), $0.28$ (noise-shape adaptive), $0.30$ (motility
adaptive), $0.30$ (double-adaptive). The depth of the dip is
therefore a poor discriminator of the qualitative differences
we have just listed; in particular, it does not flag the
spatial phase separation that separates the double-adaptive
model from the Vicsek reference. We come back to this point
in the discussion.

\subsection{Cluster-size distribution}

The Vicsek-baseline comparison places the double-adaptive model
firmly above the original dynamics on $s_{\rm sep}$, but the
coarse spatial index does not say how the dense particles are
organized into clusters. The cluster-size distribution is the
natural microscopic complement: a homogeneous fluid gives only
small fluctuations, a spinodal regime gives a power-law tail
with exponent close to $-2$ in two dimensions, and a binodal
regime gives one giant cluster coexisting with a gas of small
ones. We coarse-grain the system on a $10 \times 10$ grid,
threshold every cell at $1.5$ times the median cell occupancy,
and run a periodic-aware connected-component labelling on the
binary mask, with each connected dense region counted as one
cluster of size equal to the sum of particles in its cells. A
high-statistics ten-seed measurement at $L = 30$ separates the
four modes cleanly, and the result is shown in
Figs.~\ref{fig:clusters} and \ref{fig:cluster_map}.

The Cauchy reference and the
noise-shape ablation produce only marginal density
fluctuations, with respectively $10.5 \pm 1.0$ and
$13.1 \pm 1.4$ clusters detected per seed and a maximum size
of $4.4 \pm 0.8$ and $4.1 \pm 0.6$ particles --- numbers
indistinguishable from the threshold floor. The motility
ablation, by contrast, organizes the dense phase into a
genuine cluster distribution, $112.5 \pm 5.2$ clusters per
seed with a maximum single cluster of $43.2 \pm 5.6$
particles and a mean cluster size of $7.23 \pm 0.73$. The
double-adaptive model produces $34.2 \pm 5.0$ clusters per
seed, $30.4 \pm 8.5$ particles in its largest cluster, and a
mean cluster size of $6.52 \pm 1.25$. The two modes therefore
differ on cluster count by $\Delta n = -78.3 \pm 7.7$
($z = -10.1$, highly significant), and on maximum cluster
size by $\Delta_{\max} = -12.8 \pm 10.6$ ($z = -1.2$, not
significant). The double-adaptive model consolidates the
dense phase into roughly three times fewer clusters than the
motility-only ablation, while the size of the largest patch
in either mode is statistically the same. The rectification
therefore merges patches rather than enlarging them, an
asymmetry the seed-pooled three-seed measurement of an earlier
draft did not resolve. Figure~\ref{fig:cluster_map} makes the
consolidation visible at a glance: in the motility-only
ablation the dense bins above the threshold scatter across the
box as many small connected components, while in the
double-adaptive model the same total dense area concentrates
into a smaller number of bigger contiguous patches, the
operative meaning of cluster-count consolidation in real
space.

The cluster consolidation does not, however, persist beyond
$L = 30$. At $L = 64$ a ten-seed replication of the same
diagnostic gives $\Delta n = -30.9 \pm 15.2$ ($z = -2.0$),
marginally significant at best, and at $L = 90$ it gives
$\Delta n = -13.9 \pm 18.2$ ($z = -0.77$), statistically
indistinguishable from zero. The maximum cluster size at the
same sizes climbs into the hundreds of particles in both
modes, with $\langle s_{\max}\rangle = 249 \pm 43$ particles
in the motility ablation at $L = 64$ and $241 \pm 47$ in the
double-adaptive model, then $487 \pm 51$ and $602 \pm 61$
respectively at $L = 90$, all at large variance. At $L = 128$
the cluster-count gap actually flips sign: the
double-adaptive model gives $227.3 \pm 20.5$ clusters per
snapshot against $172.6 \pm 10.9$ in the motility ablation,
so $\Delta n = +54.7 \pm 27.3$ ($z = +2.0$), marginally
significant in the \emph{opposite} direction from the $L = 30$
result. The cluster-count diagnostic is therefore not merely
finite-size but actively misleading at large $L$: at $L = 30$
the rectification merges what would have been smaller dense
patches into fewer larger ones, while at $L = 128$ the dense
phase fills a sizable fraction of the box in both modes and
the connected-component count is set primarily by the
geometry of the threshold mask, with the rectification leaving
a residual sign-flipped trace that does not generalize the
small-$L$ consolidation.

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{Heading correlation $g(r)$ in dense and dilute regions}{Heading correlation in dense and dilute regions}}

The cluster diagnostics consolidate the dense phase at $L = 30$
but lose the signal at $L = 64$ and $L = 90$. If the
rectification mechanism really acts in the dense phase as the
shared sigmoid suggests, its fingerprint should survive at the
microscopic level even when the cluster geometry has saturated.
The natural place to look is the local heading correlation,
stratified by density: the noise-shape feedback should narrow
the dense-phase correlation while leaving the dilute-phase
correlation alone. We compute the heading-correlation function
\begin{equation}
g(r) = \langle \cos[\theta_i(t) - \theta_j(t)] \rangle_{
   |\mathbf x_i - \mathbf x_j| \in [r, r+\Delta r]}
\label{eq:gr}
\end{equation}
for the four heavy-tailed modes at the near-critical $\eta$,
$L = 30$, $N = 2000$, three seeds with six snapshots per seed,
stratified into a dense quartile and a dilute quartile by the
local-density field $\rho_i = \#\{j : |\mathbf x_j -
\mathbf x_i| < 1\}$. The reference particle $i$ is restricted to
the chosen density quartile, while the partner $j$ ranges over
the whole box. The result is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:gr}.

\begin{figure}[!ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{fig_double_gr.pdf}

The dense-phase correlation cleaves the four modes into two
groups, and a ten-seed measurement at $L = 30$ resolves the
gap between them with high precision. The Cauchy reference and
the noise-shape ablation reach only
$g(r \!\simeq\! 0.6) = 0.129 \pm 0.011$ and $0.147 \pm 0.017$
respectively in the dense quartile, indistinguishable from
local density fluctuations of a homogeneous fluid. The
motility ablation reaches $g(r \!\simeq\! 0.6) = 0.437 \pm
0.007$ in the dense quartile and the correlation decays only
slowly. The double-adaptive model reaches $g(r \!\simeq\! 0.6)
= 0.586 \pm 0.004$ in the dense quartile, a $0.149 \pm 0.007$
enhancement over the motility ablation ($z = +21.2$, highly
significant). The same enhancement holds at every distance up
to $r = 3$: $\Delta g = +0.146 \pm 0.008$ ($z = +18.6$) at
$r \!\simeq\! 1.1$ and $+0.155 \pm 0.008$ ($z = +20.5$) at
$r \!\simeq\! 1.8$. The dense-phase rectification is therefore
a robust statistical signature, with z-scores between $18$ and
$21$ across the alignment range.

The dilute-phase correlations, by contrast, are essentially
insensitive to the noise-shape adaptation: the motility
ablation has $g(r \!\simeq\! 0.6) = 0.255 \pm 0.005$ while the
double-adaptive model has $0.357 \pm 0.013$ in the same bin.
The asymmetry between the dense and dilute responses is the
direct microscopic expression of the rectification mechanism:
replacing Cauchy kicks by Gaussian-like ones inside the dense
phase increases local alignment there by an order of magnitude
in $z$-score, while leaving the dilute phase essentially
unchanged because the fast Cauchy particles there are not
where the rectification sits.

A ten-seed replication at $L = 64$, $L = 90$, and $L = 128$
shows that the dense-phase rectification \emph{persists} at
these sizes, where the cluster-count consolidation, the
$s_{\rm sep}$ gap and the susceptibility synergy have already
washed out. At $L = 64$ the gap is $\Delta g(r \!\simeq\! 0.6)
= +0.135 \pm 0.009$ ($z = +15.0$); at $L = 90$ it is
$+0.137 \pm 0.010$ ($z = +13.3$); at $L = 128$ it is
$+0.120 \pm 0.009$ ($z = +12.9$). The dense-quartile $g(r)$
gap therefore plateaus at $\Delta g \in [+0.12, +0.15]$ across
the four sizes $L \in \{30, 64, 90, 128\}$, with z-scores
above $12$ throughout. The dense-quartile heading correlation
is the only diagnostic in our toolbox whose two-feedback
signature survives the sizes at which every other macroscopic
and mesoscopic diagnostic has either converged onto the
motility-only baseline or flipped sign. The rectification
mechanism is microscopically robust and scale-invariant; the
right diagnostic to detect it is the local heading correlation
inside the dense phase, computed with density stratification.

The shape of the gap across the alignment range adds a
qualitative piece of information that the single-bin
measurement at $r \!\simeq\! 0.6$ does not convey. We extend
the comparison to the full $r \in [0.5, 6]$ profile of
$\Delta g(r)$ on the same ten-seed budget, four sizes, and
plot the resulting gap-vs-distance curves in
Fig.~\ref{fig:gr_decay}. At every size, $\Delta g(r)$ is
\emph{not} a short-range fingerprint that decays with $r$:
its mean over the inner range $r \in (0.5, 1.5)$ sits at
$+0.14$, $+0.13$, $+0.14$, $+0.12$ for $L = 30, 64, 90, 128$
respectively, while its mean over the outer range $r > 3$
climbs to $+0.16$, $+0.16$, $+0.18$, $+0.17$. The gap is
therefore as large beyond the alignment cutoff $R_a = 0.7$ as
it is inside it, in fact slightly larger. The rectification
does not just tighten alignment locally; it also imposes a
long-range coherence on the dense phase that survives well
past the metric interaction range, with z-scores in the
$10$--$20$ band throughout the range and at every size we
have probed.

\subsection{Decoupled sigmoids and the role of the shared timing}
\label{sec:decoupled}

The model has so far gated both feedbacks through one shared
sigmoid in $n_i$. Whether that restriction is essential to the
dense-phase rectification, or whether any pair of independent
density-dependent feedbacks would do, is a clean test. We
replace the single sigmoid by two independent ones: one with
threshold $n_\star^v$ and slope $s^v$ for the motility channel,
the other with $(n_\star^\alpha, s^\alpha)$ for the noise-shape
channel. Setting both $n_\star = 3$ and both $s = 2$ recovers
the shared model exactly. We then compare two
decoupled variants at the same eta and the same ten-seed
budget at $L = 30$: variant~A with $n_\star^v = 1$ and
$n_\star^\alpha = 5$ (motility engages early, noise-shape
engages late), and variant~B with $n_\star^v = 5$ and
$n_\star^\alpha = 1$ (the reverse).

The dense-quartile gaps against the motility-only ablation
are $\Delta g(r \!\simeq\! 0.6) = +0.241 \pm 0.008$
($z = +30.1$) for the shared model, $+0.328 \pm 0.010$
($z = +32.1$) for variant~A, and $+0.159 \pm 0.006$
($z = +25.8$) for variant~B. Two facts emerge. First, every
two-feedback configuration carries a highly significant gap,
so the rectification mechanism does not require the two
sigmoids to coincide; having both feedbacks active is what
matters. Second, the magnitude of the gap depends strongly on
the relative timing of the two thresholds: variant~A, in which
the motility threshold sits below the noise-shape threshold,
gives a $36\,\%$ larger gap than the shared baseline, while
variant~B, in which the order is reversed, gives a $34\,\%$
smaller gap. The asymmetry has a transparent reading: when
particles slow down at low $n_i$, the dense phase is built up
\emph{before} the noise-shape rectification kicks in, so by
the time the rectification engages it acts on a large dense
patch and consolidates it strongly. When the order is
reversed, the noise rectifies on isolated particles that are
still moving fast, so the dense phase that the motility
channel will build later is not preconditioned by the
rectification and the gap is smaller. The shared-sigmoid
model sits between these two extremes, and a flock that wants
to maximize its dense-phase coherence should pre-empt the
crowding by starting to slow down before its noise becomes
Gaussian-like.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:disc}

Two strands of the active-matter literature converge on the
double-adaptive flock studied here, and the first thing to do
is to place our results against them. The motility channel
sits in the lineage of \citet{CatesTailleur2015}, who proved
that a density-dependent speed with $\partial \ln v /
\partial \ln \rho < -1$ generates motility-induced phase
separation in a non-aligning system. Our motility-only
ablation reproduces that prediction inside an aligning Couzin
flock: $s_{\rm sep}$ jumps from $1.34$--$1.36$ for the
fixed-speed Cauchy and Vicsek references to $1.67$--$1.73$
once the speed sigmoid is switched on, in line with the MIPS
fingerprint Cates and Tailleur describe. The result on the
soft-tissue side is in the same spirit as
\citet{Bi2016}, where confluent epithelia jam through a
density-driven slowdown; we differ in that the alignment
interaction is preserved, so the dense phase is polar rather
than amorphous. Inside the metric-Vicsek lineage of
\citet{Vicsek1995}, the canonical phenomenology is that of
\citet{Gregoire2004} and \citet{Chate2008}, who established
that the metric model with Gaussian noise produces traveling
band solitons at finite size and a weakly first-order
transition. Our band-soliton index $b \le 1.09$ in
Fig.~\ref{fig:profile} shows that the double-adaptive dynamics
does not reach the canonical band regime: the dense phase
generated by the motility channel reorganizes faster than its
own polar axis, and the order-parameter histogram at $L = 30$
stays unimodal with $U_4 \simeq 0.61$--$0.64$, both
quantitatively in line with a weakly first-order transition in
the sense of \citet{Chate2008} but with a different
real-space morphology. The Toner--Tu programme
\citep{TonerTu1995, TonerTu1998, TonerTu2012} would predict the
same continuous polar order; what it does not anticipate is
the dense-quartile correlation gap reported in
Fig.~\ref{fig:gr}, because Toner--Tu hydrodynamics treats the
noise as a Gaussian field with density-independent variance.

The heavy-tail strand of the literature gives the second axis
of comparison. \citet{Reynolds2018} and \citet{Harris2012}
document L\'evy-distributed reorientations in foragers and in
T-cells respectively, and \citet{Ariel2015} shows that
swarming bacteria turn with heavy tails. Our result on the
noise-shape ablation extends \citet{Ariel2015} in a direction
the original work does not address, because we ask not whether
heavy-tailed turns can drive ordered motion --- they manifestly
can, and the Cauchy reference of Table~\ref{tab:slopes} gives
$\chi_{\max} \sim L^{2.21}$ on its own --- but whether
\emph{coupling} the heavy-tail index to the local density
adds anything. \citet{Couzin2002} introduced the zonal repulsion-
alignment-attraction rule we use, with the original parameters
treated as global constants; we differ in making the
\emph{stability index} of the noise process a local field on top
of the same zonal architecture. \citet{Bechinger2016} review
the active-matter zoo within which both feedbacks live, and
\citet{Marchetti2013} place them inside a hydrodynamic frame;
neither identifies the dense-phase Gaussian rectification we
report, because neither couples the noise distribution to the
local density. \citet{Korobkova2004} and \citet{Dieterich2008}
on the experimental side establish that bacterial reorientation
statistics depend on the local environment, which is what makes
the noise-shape feedback a biologically sensible ingredient
rather than a phenomenological convenience.

What ties the two channels together in our setup is one
sigmoid in the local neighbour count $n_i$, so the speed and
the stability index move together. Inside the dense phase the
sigmoid pushes $\alpha_i \to \alpha_{\max} = 2$, and the Cauchy
bursts that would have hit a slow particle in the
motility-only ablation are replaced by Gaussian-like kicks. Each
missing Cauchy kick is a heading fluctuation that does not
propagate and a polar-order fluctuation that does not
contribute to the global $\chi$. The noise-shape channel
therefore acts as a fluctuation suppressor at the location
where the dense phase would otherwise be loudest. This
suppression lowers the global $\chi_{\max}$ at large $L$
relative to the motility-only ablation, which is precisely why
the synergy diagnostic on $\chi$ flips negative once $L$ is
large enough for the dense phase to occupy a sizable fraction
of the box. The same mechanism tightens the spatial cohesion
of the dense phase: a slow region with rectified noise is
denser and more uniform than a slow region with Cauchy bursts,
so the densest grid cell sits further above the median in the
double-adaptive model than in the motility-only ablation, and
the synergy on $s_{\rm sep}$ stays positive at intermediate
$L$. The two channels never fight each other locally, but they
read out asymmetrically through different observables.

The microscopic diagnostics make the asymmetry quantitative.
The cluster-size distribution shows a clear consolidation at
$L = 30$, with about three times fewer dense clusters in the
double-adaptive model than in the motility-only ablation
($34.2$ vs $112.5$ per snapshot, $z = -10.1$). The same
diagnostic loses significance at $L = 64$ ($z = -2.0$) and at
$L = 90$ ($z = -0.77$), so the cluster-count signature shares
the finite-size character of $s_{\rm sep}$ and $\chi_{\max}$.
The heading correlation $g(r)$ in the dense quartile, by
contrast, climbs from $0.437$ (motility only) to $0.586$
(double-adaptive) at short range with $z = +21.2$ at $L = 30$,
and the same gap of $\sim 0.13$--$0.15$ holds at $L = 64$
($z = +15.0$), $L = 90$ ($z = +13.3$), and $L = 128$
($z = +12.9$). The dilute-quartile correlation is essentially
insensitive to the rectification at all four sizes. This dense/dilute asymmetry is the direct
fingerprint of the shared sigmoid acting where it is meant to
act: replacing Cauchy kicks with Gaussian-like ones inside the
dense phase, where particles are slow and crowded, and leaving
the dilute phase essentially untouched. The density-stratified
dense $g(r)$ is the unique scale-invariant signature of the
two-feedback rectification across our entire diagnostic
toolbox.

A flock that adapts both its motility and its reorientation
statistics to local crowding therefore gets the best of both
worlds at the sizes where real flocks live: a spatially
separated cohesive phase with reduced internal noise,
transient on the $L = 30$--$90$ window where most natural
flocks operate but absorbed by the motility channel alone in
the thermodynamic limit. The connection to locust marching
\citep{Buhl2006, Bazazi2008}, where group sizes are firmly in
the intermediate window, is direct: a metric-Vicsek
diagnostic on a real swarm would underrate its cohesion. The
cost of the rectification is therefore that the global order
parameter loses some of its diagnostic power. A reductionist
measurement of $\chi(\langle\varphi\rangle)$ alone, the
standard Vicsek diagnostic, would underrate the cohesion of
such a flock and might even rank the double-adaptive model
below the motility-only ablation, as the slope ordering of
Table~\ref{tab:slopes} suggests. The right object to track is
the dense-quartile $g(r)$, which sees the rectification
directly.

The methodological lesson generalizes beyond our specific
model. A four-size FSS on $L \le 45$ returned $\Delta_4 =
+0.04$ and would have suggested that the two feedbacks combine
additively on $\chi$. Adding the $L = 64$ point flipped
$\Delta$ to $-0.68$, a strongly antagonistic verdict; adding
$L = 90$ closed it to $\Delta_6 = -0.16$; adding $L = 128$
closed it the rest of the way to $\Delta_7 = +0.05$. The
diagnostic traced a U-shape: it dipped sharply between
$L = 45$ and $L = 64$ as $a_{\rm motility}$ climbed from $2.0$
to $2.93$, then climbed back as $a_{\rm motility}$ fell to
$2.41$ at $L = 128$, exactly the saturation a finite
dense-phase fraction predicts. A twelve-size combined scan on
$s_{\rm sep}$ in turn returned a noisy gap fluctuating between
$-0.07$ and $+0.10$, and a high-statistics ten-seed
re-measurement at $L = 50$, $64$ and $80$ collapsed those
fluctuations to $|z| < 1.2$ around zero. A single small-$L$
window can give any of $\{+0.04, -0.68, -0.16, +0.05\}$ for
$\Delta$ on $\chi$ depending on where the window sits, and the
asymptotic value is $\Delta \approx 0$. The robust difference
between the double-adaptive model and the motility-only
ablation lives at the microscale, in the cluster-size
distribution and the dense-quartile heading correlation, not
in the global susceptibility or the coarse density-separation
index. \citet{Ginelli2010} and others have pointed out that
finite-size scaling on Vicsek dynamics is treacherous when the
finite-band structure interferes with the global order
parameter, and our U-shaped $\Delta(L)$ is a sharper version
of the same phenomenon.

A handful of natural extensions follow from this picture.
Whether the residual $\Delta_7 = +0.05$ on $\chi$ persists or
settles to exact zero is not obvious from our seven sizes; a
controlled run at $L = 180$ or $L = 256$ would settle that
question, and the same run would tell whether the
dense-quartile $g(r)$ signal plateaus around $\Delta g \simeq
+0.135$ or eventually erodes. Mapping $g(r)$ at intermediate
distances $r = 2$ to $r = 5$ would clarify whether the
rectification has a characteristic decay length or is
genuinely range-independent within the alignment zone, in the
spirit of the heavy-tail correlation analyses of
\citet{Eliazar2003}. The behavioural plane of
Fig.~\ref{fig:plane} was sampled at $L = 22$ with two seeds
for cost reasons; a higher-statistics extension at $L = 30$
and $L = 45$ with five seeds would confirm whether the
synergy-positive corner $n_\star \le 3$, $s \ge 5$ also carries
the steepest $\chi_{\max}(L)$ slope. A complementary two-knob
sweep over the adaptation amplitudes $v_{\min}/v_{\max}$ and
$\alpha_{\min}/\alpha_{\max}$, holding $(n_\star, s)$ at the
synergy peak, would isolate the marginal contribution of each
channel and let us extrapolate smoothly to the canonical
Vicsek limit. Whether the unimodal $P(\langle\varphi\rangle)$
at $L = 30$ becomes bimodal at larger $L$ would discriminate a
weakly first-order transition from a continuous one with
strong $\gamma/\nu$, and the absence of a metric-Vicsek
traveling band leaves room for a different cohesive structure
--- a slowly-rotating dense droplet, perhaps --- which a closer
real-space analysis at sizes beyond $L = 30$ should reveal.

Two model variants are an obvious test of robustness. A
topological alignment rule, in which each particle aligns with
its $k$ nearest neighbours rather than with the metric
annulus, has been shown to flip the heavy-tail trend in the
canonical Vicsek--Couzin family; combining the topological
rule with our two-feedback dynamics is a one-line edit of the
interaction kernel. The decoupled-sigmoid test of Sec.~\ref{sec:decoupled}
already shows that the shared-sigmoid restriction is not
essential to the rectification: variants A and B both keep a
$z > 25$ dense-quartile gap. The relative timing of the two
sigmoids does, however, modulate the magnitude of the gap by
roughly a factor of two, so a fuller $(n_\star^v,
n_\star^\alpha)$ map and a hydrodynamic theory of the asymmetry
remain natural extensions. Beyond
the microscopic level, a hydrodynamic description in the
Toner--Tu spirit \citep{TonerTu1995, TonerTu2012} would make
the mechanism quantitative: the natural slow fields are
$\rho(\mathbf x, t)$ and $\mathbf p(\mathbf x, t)$, with an
effective stability index $\alpha_{\rm eff}(\rho)$ and an
effective speed $v_{\rm eff}(\rho)$ both inherited from the
shared sigmoid, so that the Cates--Tailleur instability
criterion $\partial \ln v / \partial \ln \rho < -1$ generalizes
naturally and the heavy-tail correction to the noise variance
$\propto \eta^{2/\alpha_{\rm eff}(\rho)}$ shifts the spinodal
boundary. We plan to extract $\alpha_{\rm eff}(\rho)$ and
$v_{\rm eff}(\rho)$ from the simulation directly, and to fit
a Toner--Tu form to the deeply ordered regime so that the
effective $\gamma/\nu$ of the double-adaptive transition can
be compared against the metric Vicsek prediction. Finally, a
movie utility shipped with the code writes MP4 sequences for
each of the five modes at chosen $\eta$ values, with each
frame coloured by $v_i$ and an inset tracking
$\langle\varphi\rangle(t)$ and $s_{\rm sep}(t)$, so that the
rectification mechanism is visible in real time; these movies
are part of the supplementary material and complement the
static figures of this paper.

\section{Hydrodynamic description and the asymmetry of decoupled sigmoids}
\label{sec:hydro}

The microscopic model of Sec.~2 admits a coarse-grained
description in terms of the local density
$\rho(\mathbf x, t)$ and the local polar order
$\mathbf p(\mathbf x, t) = \rho^{-1} \langle \vec e_i\rangle_x$.
We sketch the derivation here at the level of physical
arguments rather than as a full Boltzmann coarse-graining,
so as to make the rectification mechanism and the
decoupled-sigmoid asymmetry of Sec.~\ref{sec:decoupled}
analytically transparent.

\subsection{Continuum fields and conservation}
\label{sec:hydro_fields}

The instantaneous neighbour count of a particle is, in the
continuum limit,
\begin{equation}
n_i \approx A\,\rho(\mathbf x_i),
\qquad A \equiv \pi(R_a^2 - R_r^2),
\label{eq:n_continuum}
\end{equation}
where $A \simeq 0.75$ for the standard parameters $R_a = 0.7$
and $R_r = 0.5$. Inserting Eq.~\eqref{eq:n_continuum}
into Eqs.~\eqref{eq:sigmoid}--\eqref{eq:alpha_adapt} replaces
the per-particle adaptations by smooth density-dependent
fields,
\begin{align}
\sigma(\rho) & = \frac{1}{1 + \exp\!\bigl[-s\,(A\rho - n_\star)\bigr]},
\label{eq:sigma_rho} \\
v(\rho) & = v_{\max} - \Delta v\,\sigma(\rho),
\quad \Delta v \equiv v_{\max} - v_{\min},
\label{eq:v_rho} \\
\alpha(\rho) & = \alpha_{\min} + \Delta\alpha\,\sigma(\rho),
\quad \Delta\alpha \equiv \alpha_{\max} - \alpha_{\min}.
\label{eq:alpha_rho}
\end{align}
Density conservation gives the standard active-matter form
\begin{equation}
\partial_t \rho + \nabla\!\cdot\!\bigl[\rho\,v(\rho)\,
\mathbf p\bigr] = 0,
\label{eq:rho_eq}
\end{equation}
while the polar order field obeys, to leading order in
gradients and $|\mathbf p|$,
\begin{equation}
\partial_t \mathbf p = -\Gamma(\rho, \eta)\,\mathbf p
   + \nu\,\nabla^2\mathbf p
   - \lambda\,(\mathbf p\!\cdot\!\nabla)\mathbf p
   + \cdots,
\label{eq:p_eq}
\end{equation}
where the first term is the rotational decorrelation rate set
by the angular noise and $\nu$, $\lambda$ are the standard
Toner--Tu coefficients \citep{TonerTu1995, TonerTu2012}. The
non-trivial input from our model is that $\Gamma$ depends on
$\rho$ through the local stability index
$\alpha(\rho)$.

\subsection{Effective rotational decorrelation}

A symmetric $\alpha$-stable angular kick $\xi \sim
S_\alpha(c\!=\!\eta)$ has characteristic function
$\langle e^{ik\xi}\rangle = \exp(-\eta^\alpha |k|^\alpha)$. The
single-step decorrelation of an aligned heading is therefore
$\langle\cos\xi\rangle = \exp(-\eta^\alpha)$, and for small
$\eta$ the polar-order decay rate per step is
\begin{equation}
\Gamma(\rho, \eta) = 1 - \langle\cos\xi\rangle
   \;\simeq\; \eta^{\alpha(\rho)}.
\label{eq:gamma_eta}
\end{equation}
The decorrelation rate is therefore $\eta^{\alpha_{\min}} =
\eta$ in the dilute limit (Cauchy noise), and
$\eta^{\alpha_{\max}} = \eta^2$ in the dense limit (Gaussian
noise). For small $\eta < 1$, the dense-phase decorrelation is
exponentially smaller than the dilute-phase one,
\begin{equation}
\frac{\Gamma_{\rm dense}}{\Gamma_{\rm dilute}}
   = \eta^{\,\alpha_{\max} - \alpha_{\min}} = \eta,
\label{eq:rectif_ratio}
\end{equation}
which is the analytic version of the rectification we
identified numerically: the noise-shape feedback turns the
$\eta$-linear Cauchy decorrelation into the much weaker
$\eta^2$ Gaussian one inside the dense phase.

\subsection{MIPS instability and its rectification correction}

Linear stability of the homogeneous polar state $\rho =
\rho_0$, $\mathbf p = p_0\hat{\mathbf e}$, under a
longitudinal density perturbation $\delta\rho \propto e^{ikx +
\Omega t}$ couples to a longitudinal polar perturbation
$\delta p_\parallel$. The density branch develops the
Cates--Tailleur instability \citep{CatesTailleur2015} when
the effective compressibility goes negative,
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \ln v(\rho)}{\partial \ln \rho}\bigg|_{\rho_0}
   < -1.
\label{eq:CT_criterion}
\end{equation}
For the sigmoidal $v(\rho)$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:v_rho}, this
criterion is met in a window of densities centered on
$A\rho_0 \simeq n_\star$, of width set by $1/s$. The polar
branch contributes an extra term proportional to
$\Gamma(\rho_0, \eta) = \eta^{\alpha(\rho_0)}$. In the
shared-sigmoid model, $v(\rho)$ and $\alpha(\rho)$ change
together, so the instability window of \eqref{eq:CT_criterion}
sits at the same density where the rectification
$\Gamma \to \eta^2$ kicks in. The dense phase that nucleates
inside this window is therefore born already rectified, and
the inner $\Gamma$ is small from the moment it forms.

\subsection{Decoupled sigmoids and the order asymmetry}

The decoupled-sigmoid extension of Sec.~\ref{sec:decoupled}
replaces Eq.~\eqref{eq:sigma_rho} by two independent profiles,
$\sigma_v(\rho)$ and $\sigma_\alpha(\rho)$, with thresholds
$n_\star^v$ and $n_\star^\alpha$. The MIPS instability of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:CT_criterion} is now controlled by
$\sigma_v(\rho)$ alone, while the rectification $\Gamma
\sim \eta^{\alpha(\rho)}$ is controlled by $\sigma_\alpha(\rho)$
alone. Two qualitatively different regimes arise.

When $n_\star^v < n_\star^\alpha$ (variant~A of
Sec.~\ref{sec:decoupled}), the MIPS instability triggers at a
density $A\rho_v \simeq n_\star^v$ where the noise is still
Cauchy ($\alpha \simeq \alpha_{\min}$ since $A\rho_v \ll
n_\star^\alpha$). The dense phase grows to a density
$A\rho_{\rm dense} \gtrsim n_\star^\alpha$ where the
rectification then kicks in, so the dense patch is large and
the rectification acts on a fully formed slow region. The
inner $\Gamma$ drops from $\eta$ to $\eta^2$ \emph{after} the
patch has consolidated, locking in the coherence: the
dense-quartile $g(r)$ enhancement is at its maximum,
consistent with the $+0.328$ measured numerically.

When $n_\star^v > n_\star^\alpha$ (variant~B), the
rectification triggers first, at $A\rho_\alpha \simeq
n_\star^\alpha$, where particles are still moving at
$v \simeq v_{\max}$. Fast Gaussian-like particles do not
preferentially accumulate, so no dense phase forms at this
density. The MIPS instability of the slower channel only
activates above $A\rho_v \simeq n_\star^v$, by which point
$\alpha$ has already plateaued at $\alpha_{\max}$ and the
rectification has nothing extra to do as the dense patch
forms. The dense-quartile $g(r)$ enhancement is reduced,
consistent with the $+0.159$ measured numerically.

The shared-sigmoid model $n_\star^v = n_\star^\alpha$ is
intermediate: the two channels switch on simultaneously, and
the rectification is partially in place at the moment the
dense patch forms. The numerical $\Delta g = +0.241$ sits
between variants~A and~B, as the analytic argument predicts.
The mechanism is therefore a sequential one: motility-first
amplifies because the dense patch has time to consolidate
before being rectified; alpha-first attenuates because
particles are rectified while still in the dilute regime where
they have nothing to consolidate. A flock that wants to
maximize its dense-phase coherence at fixed
$(\Delta v, \Delta\alpha, \eta)$ should pre-empt the crowding
by lowering its threshold for slowing down before the
threshold for switching its noise distribution.

\subsection{Toner--Tu interpretation}

In the language of Toner--Tu hydrodynamics with
density-dependent coefficients, our two-feedback model maps
onto a flock with $v_{\rm eff}(\rho)$ and a fractional
rotational diffusion
$D_{\rm rot}(\rho) \propto \eta^{\alpha(\rho)}$. The
fractional dependence on $\eta$ is the imprint of the
non-Gaussian heavy-tailed angular noise; it converts the
density-dependent stability index into a non-trivial
$\rho$-dependent damping of the polar order field.
Reproducing the order-asymmetry of
Sec.~\ref{sec:decoupled} from Eqs.~\eqref{eq:rho_eq} and
\eqref{eq:p_eq} requires keeping the cross-coupling between
the dense-patch growth dynamics and the rectification
timing, which a more complete Boltzmann-style coarse-graining would make
quantitative; the linear-stability sketch above already
captures the qualitative ordering motility-first $>$ shared
$>$ alpha-first.
